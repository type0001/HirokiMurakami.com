How to use this

Swapping

Every block has a “Swap this” link. Open it to see the alternates already researched for that slot — same neighbourhood, same time of day. Swapping one block never breaks the rest of the day.

Splitting

Blocks are marked Together or Split. Split blocks always have a shopping option and a sitting-down-with-coffee option within a block or two of each other, plus a stated time and place to regroup.

Opting out

Every day has a low-effort exit. If someone wants a morning in bed or needs to eat at the hotel, the plan absorbs it — the market morning, the roots morning, and one booked dinner are the only fixed points.

The kitchen

Le Nomade is an aparthotel — the units have full kitchens. That's why Saturday's dinner is a cook-in from Jean-Talon, and why anyone with a restricted menu has an easy fallback any night.

The French Montréal thread

If we want to make the roots part real rather than atmospheric, these are the places that hold actual records and actual buildings.

BAnQ Vieux-Montréal — the archives
535 avenue Viger Est, ten minutes on foot. The primary destination for French-Canadian genealogy: Quebec civil-status registers, parish baptisms, marriages and burials to 1917, notarial records, marriage contracts, censuses, city directories. Free, no appointment, 200-seat reading room. Open Tue/Fri/Sat 9–5 and Wed/Thu 9–9 — closed Sunday and Monday, so it has to be Friday or Saturday. Worth doing the online prep first and arriving with names and approximate dates.
Maison Saint-Gabriel
2146 Place Dublin, Pointe-Saint-Charles. A 1660s fieldstone farmhouse bought by Marguerite Bourgeoys in 1668 that housed the Filles du Roi — the best surviving picture of how the first French families actually lived here. Costumed guides. Open Wed–Fri afternoons and weekends 10–5.
Notre-Dame-des-Neiges Cemetery
On Mount Royal, the largest cemetery in Canada and where generations of francophone Montrealers are buried. Only worth the half-day if we go in with surnames.
Already on the plan
Pointe-à-Callière (Friday) sits on the founding site itself. Notre-Dame Basilica and the Sulpician Old Seminary next door are the institutional heart of French Catholic Montréal. Château Ramezay covers colonial governance, and the McCord Stewart Museum downtown holds the city's social-history photography.

Things worth knowing

Getting around

Champ-de-Mars is two minutes from the door; Place-d'Armes is five. A single fare is C$3.75, a 24-hour pass about C$11.50, three days about C$22. Buy an OPUS card at any station — with six people hopping across the city, the passes usually win.

Paying & tipping

Tipping works like home: 15–20%. Tax of roughly 15% is added at the register, not shown on the menu. For a table of six, many places add 15–18% automatically — check before tipping twice. Patati Patata and Café Olimpico skew cash.

Language

Open with bonjour and staff will switch to English immediately. No French needed to get by, but it's the courteous move and it fits why we're here.

A table for six

The main friction of the trip. Plenty of rooms cap online booking at four or six, or want a phone call above that. For the walk-in places, either arrive at opening or split into two threes and push the tables together.

Groceries

Old Montreal genuinely has none. The IGA at Complexe Desjardins (150 Ste-Catherine O.) is a 10–12 minute walk with no metro needed. The Chinatown grocers on De la Gauchetière are closer but smaller.

Eating carefully

Bar George, Mon Lapin and Arthur's all have real vegetable-forward and lighter options, not just a token salad. Saturday's cook-in is the full-control night, and the hotel kitchen is the fallback on any other.

Prices, hours and opening days drift. Reconfirm Crew Collective's Thursday closure, museum hours, the Bota Bota booking and the exact Piknic Sunday dates before anything gets locked in.
